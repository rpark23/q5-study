# Lecture 02: Stimulants and Sedatives

## GABA Agonists Overview

### 3 Major Effects of GABA Agonism
1. **Sedation/calming** – used short-term for anxiety disorders or during procedures
2. **"Hypnosis"/Sleep/Unconsciousness** – insomnia treatment
3. **Anesthesia/respiratory depression/amnesia** – adjunct in anesthetics

---

## Sedatives by Class (Mildest → Most Potent)

### 1. Z-Class Medications (Mildest)
- **Example:** Zolpidem (Ambien)
- **Selectivity:** Selective for the **alpha subunit** of GABA receptor
- **Indication:** Insomnia
- **Note:** Most commonly prescribed sleeping agent

### 2. Benzodiazepines (Medium)
- **Binding site:** Multiple binding sites, increases channel affinity to GABA
- **Examples:** Lorazepam, Diazepam, Valium

#### 3 Main Properties (all due to GABA activity):
1. Anxiolysis/sedation
2. Anticonvulsant properties
3. Muscle relaxant properties

#### 3 Problems with Chronic Use:
1. **Tolerance**
2. **Withdrawal** (insomnia, anxiety, increased HR, possible seizures)
3. **Abuse potential**

### 3. Barbiturates (Most Potent)
- **MOA:** Open the chloride channel **without need for endogenous GABA**
- **Indication:** Anesthesia (but rarely used)

### Alcohol
- Also binds GABA receptor (specific site covered in images)

## Stimulants

### General Mechanism
Stimulants mimic **catecholamine (epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine) activity**

### Two Broad Classes
1. **Releasers**
2. **Reuptake inhibitors**

---

## Stimulant Medications

| Drug | MOA |
|------|-----|
| **Amphetamines / Methylphenidate** | Reverse the reuptake pump + increase vesicular release of NE |
| **Modafinil / Armodafinil** | Increased NE and DA effect (MOA not entirely clear) |
| **Cocaine** | Potent DA reuptake inhibitor |
| **Atomoxetine** | Reuptake inhibitor of norepinephrine (selective NRI) |
